Methodology

How the framework works

Four stages, each mapping to one objective in the brief — from raw pixels to an actionable, physics-grounded cooling plan.

← Back to overview
Objective 1

🗺️ Identify urban heat hotspots

Turn satellite thermal imagery into a clean, city-wide heat-stress map and automatically flag the hotspots.

  • Retrieve LST: compute Land Surface Temperature from Landsat 8 thermal bands (split-window / mono-window) and cross-check with high-resolution ECOSTRESS.
  • Correct & fuse: emissivity correction using NDVI, cloud masking, and spatio-temporal fusion to fill gaps and sharpen resolution.
  • Compute heat-stress index: combine LST with air temperature and humidity (from ERA5/CPCB) into a human-relevant heat-stress layer, not just surface temperature.
  • Flag hotspots: statistical thresholding + spatial clustering (e.g. Getis-Ord Gi*) to delineate significant hot zones.

Objective 2

🔍 Analyze the drivers of heating

Not all hot pixels are hot for the same reason. We quantify why each area heats up so interventions target the real cause.

  • Assemble a feature stack per pixel: land use/land cover, vegetation (NDVI), built-up density & height (urban morphology), impervious fraction, albedo, distance to water, and atmospheric state.
  • Attribute with explainable ML: use SHAP values on the trained model to rank each driver's contribution to temperature — globally and locally per neighborhood.
  • Output driver maps: "this block is hot mainly due to low vegetation + high impervious cover," giving planners a diagnosis, not just a symptom.

Objective 3

🧠 Model heat dynamics with physics-informed ML

The core innovation: an ML model that learns the LST↔drivers relationship and obeys the physics of surface heat exchange, so it can predict the effect of changes it never saw in training.

Base learner

A gradient-boosted / neural regressor maps the driver feature stack to LST with high accuracy on observed data — the data-driven backbone.

The physics constraint

We add a surface energy-balance term to the loss function. Net radiation must equal the sum of sensible heat, latent heat (evapotranspiration), and ground heat flux:

Rn = H + LE + G

The model is penalized when its predictions violate this balance. Vegetation and water increase latent-heat (LE) cooling; high-albedo surfaces cut absorbed radiation. Because these levers are baked into the physics, the model's counterfactual predictions ("what if we add trees here?") stay physically credible.
  • Loss = data fit + λ · physics residual, trained jointly (a PINN-style formulation).
  • Validation: spatial hold-out (train on some districts, test on others) and temporal hold-out (train on one date, test another) to prove generalization, not memorization.
  • Metrics: RMSE / MAE on LST plus a physics-consistency score.

Objective 4

🌳 Generate & optimize cooling scenarios

With a physically-grounded model, every intervention becomes a testable "what-if." We search for the best mix.

  • Simulate: edit the driver layers (raise vegetation, flip roofs to high-albedo, add a water body) and re-predict LST to get a °C reduction map.
  • Optimize under budget: treat placement as a constrained optimization — maximize total heat-stress relief (weighted toward vulnerable populations) subject to an intervention budget/area.
  • Rank actions: output a prioritized list — intervention type, exact location, and estimated °C drop.
